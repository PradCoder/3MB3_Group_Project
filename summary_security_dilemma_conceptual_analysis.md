# Summary: The Security Dilemma: A Conceptual Analysis
**Shiping Tang (2009) — Security Studies, Vol. 18(3), pp. 587–623**

---

## Core Argument
Decades of scholarship have stretched, misapplied, and confused the security dilemma. Tang rigorously reconstructs the concept from its original sources (Butterfield, Herz, Jervis — the "BHJ formulation"), derives its essential implications, critiques influential but flawed extensions, and proposes a cleaner conceptual framework. The paper also sketches a new research agenda for understanding what regulates the security dilemma's severity.

---

## The Original Three Theorists: Areas of Agreement and Difference

| Aspect | Butterfield | Herz | Jervis |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ultimate source is anarchy | No (attributes it to "universal sin") | Yes | Yes |
| Uncertainty over intentions | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| Lack of malign intentions required | Yes, but inconsistent | Yes | Yes, but inconsistent |
| Unintended, tragic results | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| Self-defeating results | Implicit | Yes | Yes |
| Both material & psychological regulators | Only psychological | Not emphasized | Yes |
| Security dilemma is universal | Yes | No — conditional | No — conditional |
| Cause of ALL wars? | Yes | No | No |

**Key problem**: None of the three provided a single, rigorous, systematic definition in one place, inviting confusion and misuse.

---

## The BHJ Formulation: A Rigorous Redefinition

A genuine security dilemma requires **three essential conditions** (all necessary):

1. **Anarchy** — no overarching sovereign authority; states must rely on self-help.
2. **No malign intentions on either side** — both states are *defensive realist* states; neither intends to threaten the other.
3. **Some accumulation of power (including offensive capability)** — because purely defensive capability is rare, even defensive buildups appear threatening.

**Complete causal chain:**
> Anarchy → need for self-help → uncertainty & fear → power competition → security dilemma activated → spiral → (potentially) war or self-defeating arms race

**Eight aspects of the security dilemma:**
1. Ultimate source: anarchy.
2. Uncertainty and fear about present and future intentions.
3. Unintentional in origin — only exists between two defensive realist states.
4. States accumulate power (including offensive capability) for defense.
5. Dynamics are self-reinforcing (spiral-like).
6. Tends to be self-defeating (more power, less security).
7. Can produce tragic results (unnecessary war).
8. Severity is regulated by material and psychological factors.

---

## Critical Implications of the Redefinition

- **Conditional, not universal**: A security dilemma only exists between two *defensive realist* states. When one or both sides have malign intentions, it is NOT a security dilemma — it is a different kind of conflict.
- **Structural, not perceptual, in origin**: Anarchy generates uncertainty structurally. Psychological factors (misperceptions) are *regulators*, not root causes.
- **Lack of malign intentions is the most diagnostic ingredient**: Because anarchy and power are almost always present, determining whether a genuine security dilemma exists turns critically on whether states are intentionally threatening each other.
- **Not all spirals are security dilemmas; not all wars are caused by the security dilemma.**

---

## Conflict of Interest and the Security Dilemma

Tang maps the complex relationship between **objective vs. subjective conflict of interest** and the security dilemma:

- **No objective conflict + subjectively reconcilable** → Security dilemma may apply; actual conflict very unlikely.
- **No objective conflict + subjectively irreconcilable** → If due to misperception (not malign intent), security dilemma applies; conflict is likely but avoidable.
- **Objective conflict + subjectively reconcilable** → Security dilemma applies but may remain dormant.
- **Objective conflict + subjectively irreconcilable** → Security dilemma generally does **not** apply; one or both sides likely malign.

Key takeaway: The security dilemma is **compatible with genuine conflict of interest** but does not require it. Defensive realism does not depend solely on misperception to explain conflict.

## Critique of Flawed Extensions

### 1. Structural vs. Perceptual Security Dilemma (Snyder 1985)
- Snyder's two types are really two different *regulators* (material vs. psychological), not two different kinds of dilemma.
- Misleadingly implies misperception is a *necessary* condition; it is not.

### 2. Imperialist's Security Dilemma (Snyder 1985)
- Describes a situation where one state has expansionist (malign) goals → **not a genuine security dilemma** by the BHJ formulation.
- Requires only "willingness to compromise," which is insufficient.

### 3. Deliberate Security Dilemma (Wheeler & Booth 1992)
- First scenario (status quo state adopts deliberately offensive posture to deter) → already accommodated by BHJ; no new concept needed.
- Second scenario (revisionist state feigns benign intent) → NOT a security dilemma; one side is malign.

### 4. State-induced Security Dilemma (Collins 2000)
- Hybrid of the above flawed concepts. An "aspiring hegemonic" power that intentionally intimidates neighbours is an **offensive realist state** — no security dilemma applies.

### 5. Always-Secure State (Glaser 1992)
- Glaser's notion of a state that is "never made insecure" by another's capabilities implicitly assumes certainty of intentions under anarchy — which severs the core causal link of the security dilemma.
- By phasing out "intentions" and substituting "security-seeking motives," Glaser makes the concept universal rather than conditional — violating BHJ.

### Common errors across all extensions:
1. Omitting a lack of malign intentions.
2. Replacing it with tractable but invalid substitutes (illusory incompatibility, security-seeking motives).
3. Conflating regulators with essential elements (over-psychologizing).
4. Conflating possible outcomes (spirals, arms races) with causes.
5. Shortening the causal chain from anarchy to dilemma.
6. Unnecessarily expanding the concept.

---

## Two Major Remedies

### Remedy 1: Distinguish the Security Dilemma from the Spiral
- A **spiral** = any continuously worsening dynamic between states (driven by self-reinforcing processes). It says nothing about whether intentions are benign or malign.
- A **security dilemma** = a spiral specifically caused by mutual defensive intent under anarchy.
- An upward spiral can be driven by: (a) a genuine security dilemma, (b) an imperialist/expansionist threat, or (c) a mutual deadlock between two malign states.
- **Not all spirals are security dilemmas. Not all security dilemmas produce spirals leading to war.**

### Remedy 2: The Security Dilemma and Spiral as a Reversible, Graduated Continuum
- States begin in a **dormant** security dilemma.
- Accumulation of power *activates* it → escalates to a **vicious/deep** security dilemma.
- If one or both sides' intentions shift from benign to malign (out of fear or provocation), the security dilemma **transforms into a spiral** — "imperialist threat" (one side malign) or "deadlock" (both malign).
- This transformation is **reversible**: if an expansionist state changes course, the situation reverts to a (deep) security dilemma.
- Applied to the Cold War: the early Cold War was a **spiral** (Stalin was genuinely expansionist); post-Stalin, it became a genuine — if deep — **security dilemma**.

## Research Agenda: Regulators of the Security Dilemma

**Existing material regulators (per Jervis):**
- Geography/proximity
- Polarity
- Military technology (offence-defence balance)
- Distinguishability of offensive vs. defensive weapons

**Additional material regulators proposed:**
- Asymmetric power distribution (great power vs. smaller neighbours)
- External actors / allies (can exacerbate spirals, as seen in ethnic conflicts)
- Mixing or concentration of ethnic groups across borders

**Psychological regulators deserving more attention:**
- Fear (undertheorized despite its prominence in realism)
- Loss aversion / prospect theory
- Ethnocentrism, worst-case forecasting, implicit enemy imaging (Wheeler & Booth)
- Reputation and credibility concerns
- Domestic politics and regime type (democracy's transparency may signal resolve but also hard-line rhetoric)

---

## Broader Significance
- The security dilemma is the **theoretical linchpin of defensive realism** — it explains why cooperation between states is possible even under anarchy.
- Correctly understood, it generates more calibrated policy prescriptions: neither pure deterrence nor pure reassurance works in all situations; the appropriate response depends on whether the situation is a genuine security dilemma, an imperialist threat, or a deadlock.
- Tang argues the world has evolved from an offensive-realist world (where most states are "wolves") toward a defensive-realist world (where most states are defensive). This makes the security dilemma *more* relevant for managing international politics, not less.
