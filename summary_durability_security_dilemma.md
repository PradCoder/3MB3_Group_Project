# Summary: The Durability of the Security Dilemma
**Jakobsen & Halvorsen (2018) — Chinese Journal of International Politics, Vol. 11(2), pp. 153–192**

---

## Core Argument
The security dilemma — the self-defeating spiral of arms competition among states that merely want security — did not end with the Cold War. Using a large cross-national dataset (150 countries, 1988–2014), the authors empirically demonstrate that action–reaction dynamics in military spending remain a prominent feature of the international system throughout the post-Cold War period, with effects strengthening notably after 2008.

---

## Theoretical Background

**What is the security dilemma?**
- Coined by John Herz; elaborated by Jervis and Glaser.
- Under anarchy, states cannot know others' intentions with certainty, so they arm for self-defense — which threatens others — causing a self-reinforcing spiral.
- The tragedy is structural: even purely status-quo states end up in arms races.
- Rooted in a Hobbesian logic: anarchy → fear → power-seeking → mutual insecurity.

**Two models of arms competition:**
1. **Deterrence model**: rational build-up to deter revisionist ("greedy") states — not a true dilemma.
2. **Security dilemma (spiral) model**: mutual, self-defeating arming among status-quo states.

**Key modifiers of severity:**
- **Intentions/motives**: greedy vs. status-quo states.
- **Geography/proximity**: nearby states threaten more; most wars are between neighbors.
- **Balance of power (polarity)**: unipolarity (Pax Americana) dampened competition; its erosion post-2008 likely revived it.
- **Offence–defence balance**: defence dominance reduces spirals; if offence and defence can be differentiated, states can signal benign intent.

**Virtuous cycles**: reciprocal disarmament is theoretically possible via repeated-game logic (tit-for-tat), democratic peace, or improved information flow. The European Union is cited as a partial empirical case.

## Research Design

| Element | Details |
|---|---|
| **Unit of analysis** | Country-year |
| **Countries** | 150 |
| **Period** | 1988–2014 |
| **Method** | Time-series cross-section; maximum likelihood estimation |
| **Dependent variable (Milex)** | Year-on-year changes in military expenditure (3-year moving averages; logged and dummy versions) — SIPRI data |
| **Key independent variable (Milexneighb)** | Weighted average of arms-spending changes among neighbours, defined using the COW Type-2 contiguity measure (land borders + ≤12 nautical miles of water); weighted by neighbours' total spending to capture power differentials |
| **Control variables** | GDP growth, natural resource rents, current defence burden (% GDP), involvement in war (≥1,000 battle deaths), and in extended models: GDP per capita, trade, regime type (Polity IV), EU membership, terrorism, military conscription, US troop presence, and power-difference dummies |

The spatial weights matrix was updated annually to account for border changes (Soviet breakup, German reunification, etc.).

---

## Key Findings

1. **Milexneighb is consistently positive and statistically significant** across all model specifications (base and extended, raw/logged/dummy versions). States do adjust their military budgets in response to neighbours' military spending changes.

2. **Both vicious and virtuous cycles exist**: when neighbours increase spending, states follow; when neighbours decrease, states also tend to decrease. The dilemma cuts both ways.

3. **Action–reaction patterns hold across the entire post-Cold War period**, but are **particularly strong for 2008–2014** — consistent with the weakening of US unipolarity, Russia's 2008 war with Georgia, and China's increased assertiveness.

4. **The dummy version of Milexneighb has the strongest effect**, suggesting states respond primarily to the *direction* (up/down) of neighbours' spending rather than exact magnitudes — consistent with defensive dominance and Jervis's predictions.

5. **Proximity and power matter**: the effect is most pronounced when neighbours are powerful. EU member states are a notable exception — their spending is driven by economic growth, not security competition.

6. **Nuclear weapons do not cancel the dilemma** — the stability-instability paradox allows conventional arms competition to persist.

7. **Liberal peace variables (democracy, trade, US troop presence) are largely insignificant** as determinants of arms-spending changes, though EU membership weakly dampens increases.

---

## Conclusions

- The security dilemma is **alive and well** in the post-Cold War era.
- It is a **general, structural phenomenon**: it operates across regions, regime types, and power configurations (with the EU as the main exception).
- The results are **pessimistic** in the realist sense — security competition appears to be a durable property of the anarchic international system.
- Future research should examine other manifestations of the security dilemma beyond military spending: alliance formation, diplomatic signalling, geoeconomic strategies, and cyberwarfare.
